Data

We will also present a brief simulation study.

K = c(5, 10, 15, 20, 25, 30)
mcycle %<>% mutate(times0 = jitter(times,
                                   amount = .01))
eval_basis = mcycle %>% {seq(from = min(.$times0),
                             to = max(.$times0),
                             length.out = 1e3 + 1)}
mcycle_fda = 
  lapply(X = seq_along(K),
         FUN = function(x){
           design_basis = create.bspline.basis(rangeval = range(mcycle$times0),
                                               norder = 4,
                                               nbasis = K[x])
           design_matrix = design_basis %>%
             getbasismatrix(evalarg = mcycle$times0,
                            basisobj = .,
                            nderiv = 0)
           design_matrixf = design_basis %>%
             getbasismatrix(evalarg = eval_basis,
                            basisobj = .,
                            nderiv = 0)
           coefficients = design_matrix %>%
             {solve(a = t(.) %*% .,
                    b = t(.) %*% mcycle$accel)}
           colnames(design_matrix) = 1:K[x]
           colnames(design_matrixf) = 1:K[x]
           return(list(K = K[x],
                       design_matrix = design_matrix,
                       design_matrixf = design_matrixf,
                       coefficients = coefficients,
                       fitted_values = design_matrix %*% coefficients,
                       eval_values = design_matrixf %*% coefficients))
           })

Setup

This vignette is primarily concerned with presenting an approach to sparse Functional Data representation utilizing the Automatic Relevance Determination (ARD) framework, itself only a topic in the wider Sparse Bayesian Learning (SBL) context. More precisely, our aim herein is to replicate some of the studies in Sousa, Souza, and Dias (2024) and Cruz, Souza, and Sousa (2024) (the former moreso than the latter, as we do not account for any correlation structure in the curves). The goal of these previous studies was to provide an adaptive Bayesian procedure to select the bases for functional data representation, the latter also extending the results of the former by including a correlation structure for the functional data, and delineating an estimation procedure utilizing the Variational Bayes (VB) approach. It did so by including a collection of Bernoulli random variables (denoted \(Z_{ki}\)), each associated with one particular term in the basis expansion in Equation 1 in the form \(\tilde{\beta}_{ki} = Z_{ki} \beta_{ki}\), such that \(\mathbb{P}(\tilde{\beta}_{ki} = 0) > 0\).

\[ \mathbf{\beta} \sim \text{Normal}(0_K,\textbf{A}). \] where \(\textbf{A} = \text{diag}(\alpha_i)\). For further detailing and intuition on the inner workings of the ARD approach, we refer the reader to Tipping (2001)

\[ \begin{aligned} \Sigma & = (\textbf{A} + \beta \Phi^\top \Phi)^{-1} \\ & = \textbf{A}^{-1} - \textbf{A}^{-1}\Phi^\top(\mathbb{I}_N + \beta \Phi \textbf{A}^{-1} \Phi^\top )^{-1}\Phi \textbf{A}^{-1} \end{aligned} \]
